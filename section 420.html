What does Section 420 IPC say?

Section 420 IPC deals with the offence of cheating and dishonestly inducing delivery of property. In simple words, it punishes a person who cheats someone and, by doing so, causes the other person to deliver property or to make, alter or destroy a valuable security.

Plain-English definition

An act falls under Section 420 when:

  • There is a dishonest or fraudulent intention (mens rea) to cheat, and
  • The accused uses a deceptive act, representation, or inducement, and
  • Because of that deception, the victim delivers property or creates/destroys/changes a valuable security — suffering loss or risk of loss.

Punishment under Section 420

Section 420 prescribes:

  • Imprisonment which may extend to (note: format numbers: 'seven'→ should be numeric? Keep numeric for clarity) — corrected: imprisonment which may extend to seven years, and
  • Fine.

The actual sentence depends on case facts, conviction, and judicial discretion.

Key elements of Section 420

  1. Cheating: Dishonest inducement or deception as defined under Section 415 IPC.
  2. Delivery or inducement: The deception must cause the victim to deliver property or create/alter/destroy a valuable security.
  3. Dishonest intention: The act must be done dishonestly — mere mistake or false promise without dishonest intention may not attract Section 420.

Simple examples

Example A (Online fraud): A creates a fake online store, takes payment from buyers, and never delivers goods. Victims pay and suffer loss. → Section 420 applies.

Example B (False promise): B falsely promises to transfer property to C and obtains money by dishonest means with no intention to transfer. → Section 420 may apply if dishonest intention is proved.

Example C (Business dispute): A business partner misrepresents accounts and induces the other to hand over assets. → If dishonest inducement is proved, Section 420 may apply.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

What is Section 420 IPC?
Section 420 punishes cheating and dishonestly inducing a person to deliver property or alter/destroy valuable security.
What is the punishment under Section 420?
Imprisonment which may extend to seven years, and a fine.
Is Section 420 a bailable offence?
Often treated as non-bailable in serious fraud cases; bail depends on facts, value involved, and court discretion.
How is Section 420 different from a civil breach of contract?
Section 420 requires dishonest intention to cheat. A civil breach without dishonesty typically leads to civil remedies, not criminal liability.
