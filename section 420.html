Offences Against Property (Fraud)

Section 420 IPC

Cheating and Dishonestly Inducing Delivery of Property

What does Section 420 cover?

Section 420 is the most commonly charged section for severe cheating offences. It deals with situations where a person commits **cheating** (defined in Section 415) and, as a direct result of that deception, causes the victim to suffer a significant loss.

The offense is committed when a person, having cheated another, dishonestly induces the person to **deliver any property** or to **make, alter, or destroy the whole or any part of a valuable security**.

Essential Elements for Section 420

The prosecution must prove two main components:

  1. **Cheating (Deception + Dishonesty):** The accused must have had a dishonest or fraudulent intention from the very beginning.
  2. **Inducement and Loss:** The cheating must directly cause the victim to:
    • Deliver property (money, goods, assets, etc.), **OR**
    • Change/destroy a valuable security (e.g., a contract, title deed, or will).

Punishment Prescribed

  • **Imprisonment** which may extend to **seven years**, and
  • **Fine**.

Because of the severity of the potential sentence, this offence is viewed seriously by the courts.

Key Facts at a Glance

Feature Details
Nature of Offence Cognizable, Non-bailable, Non-compoundable (typically)
Trial Court Court of Session or Magistrate of the First Class (depending on jurisdiction and value)
Relevant Sections Section 415 (Definition of Cheating), Section 417 (Punishment for simple Cheating), Section 420 (Aggravated Cheating involving property/valuable security)

Distinction: Cheating (417) vs. Cheating & Delivery (420)

The difference lies in the consequence:

  • **Section 417 (Simple Cheating):** Punishes cheating where the consequence is merely harm or likelihood of harm (non-monetary). Max punishment is 1 year.
  • **Section 420 (Aggravated Cheating):** Requires the cheating to result specifically in the **delivery of property or valuable security**. Max punishment is 7 years.

Simple Examples

Example A (Job Fraud):

A promises B a government job, falsely claiming influence. B pays A ₹2 lakhs. A never had the intention or ability to deliver the job and keeps the money. **Section 420 applies.**

Example B (Real Estate/Financial Fraud):

C induces D to invest in a scheme by showing fraudulent balance sheets. D hands over title deeds to his property as security for the investment. **Section 420 applies** (inducing delivery of valuable security).

Key Legal Consideration

  • **The Test of Intention:** A key defense in a Section 420 case is proving that the transaction was merely a business failure (a civil breach of contract) and not a criminal act. The prosecution must definitively prove the **dishonest intention existed at the time of the promise or inducement**.
  • **Non-Bailable Nature:** Due to the risk of financial fraud and absconding, courts frequently treat Section 420 as non-bailable, particularly in cases involving high amounts or multiple victims.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the punishment under Section 420?

Imprisonment up to seven years, along with a fine.

Is Section 420 a bailable offence?

No. It is generally a **non-bailable** offence, especially when the property delivered is of significant value or when there is strong evidence of prior dishonest intention.

How is this different from a civil dispute?

A civil breach of contract is an inability to perform. Section 420 requires the **intent to cheat** to have been present at the time the property was taken. If the intent was honest initially, it remains a civil dispute.
