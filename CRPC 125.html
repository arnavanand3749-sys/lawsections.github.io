What does Section 125 CrPC do?

Section 125 CrPC provides a quick, summary remedy for maintenance (money support) to certain persons who are unable to maintain themselves. It is meant to prevent destitution and vagrancy by obliging able persons to support their dependants.

Who can claim maintenance?

  • Wife (including divorced or separated wives in some circumstances);
  • Children (legitimate or illegitimate) who are unable to maintain themselves;
  • Parents who are unable to maintain themselves (including widowed or divorced parents);
  • In some cases, a person treated as wife or dependent relatives may also claim under similar principles.

Basic requirements

To succeed in a Section 125 claim, the claimant must generally show:

  1. The claimant is one of the persons covered by the section (wife/child/parent).
  2. The claimant is unable to maintain themselves.
  3. The respondent (usually husband/father/son) has sufficient means to pay maintenance.
  4. There is no valid reason for the respondent's refusal to maintain (e.g., the claimant is living in adultery or is capable of earning but refuses).

How much maintenance is awarded?

Court decides on a case-by-case basis considering factors like:

  • Income and means of the respondent;
  • Reasonable needs of the claimant (food, clothing, education for children, medical needs);
  • Standard of living previously enjoyed;
  • Any other sources of income available to claimant.

Courts aim for fair and reasonable maintenance — not punishment or enrichment.

Procedure

  1. Claim is filed in the Magistrate's Court (summary proceeding).
  2. Court issues notice to the respondent and conducts a brief hearing.
  3. Evidence (oral/documentary) is taken and court decides whether maintenance is payable and the quantum.
  4. Maintenance orders are enforceable; failure to comply can lead to attachment of property or imprisonment in extreme cases (summary contempt-like procedure).

Simple examples

Example A: A wife is deserted by her husband and has no income. She files under Section 125. If the husband has means, court may order monthly maintenance.

Example B: An elderly parent with no means claims maintenance from an adult child who works. Court can order reasonable monthly support.

Landmark cases & legal notes

  • Shivkant Shukla v. State of U.P. — emphasised the protective nature of Section 125 and the need to prevent vagrancy.
  • Shiv Raj v. State of Haryana — courts look at reasonable needs and respondent's means when fixing quantum.
  • Section 125 is civil in effect but a summary criminal procedure — courts adopt a liberal approach in favour of maintenance claimants.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

Who can claim maintenance under Section 125 CrPC?
Wife, children (legitimate or illegitimate) who cannot maintain themselves, and parents who are unable to maintain.
Is Section 125 only for married wives?
No. Courts have sometimes allowed claims from wives separated or divorced in certain circumstances; there are also other statutory remedies for divorced spouses.
Can the maintenance order be modified?
Yes. Either party can approach the court later to vary the quantum if there is a material change in circumstances (increase/decrease in income or needs).
What happens if respondent refuses to pay?
The court can enforce the order through attachment, garnishee, or in extreme cases, imprisonment for non-compliance under the summary procedure.
