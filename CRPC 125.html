Code of Criminal Procedure (CrPC)

Section 125 CrPC

Order for Maintenance of Wives, Children, and Parents

What is the purpose of Section 125 CrPC?

Section 125 of the Code of Criminal Procedure is a crucial piece of social legislation designed to provide a **summary, speedy, and simple remedy** for maintenance. Its primary goal is to **prevent destitution and vagrancy** by ensuring financial support for certain vulnerable dependants.

This section is a protective measure forcing a person with sufficient means to support their immediate dependants who are unable to maintain themselves.

Who can Claim Maintenance?

The following individuals are eligible to seek maintenance under this section:

  • **Wife:** Includes a legally divorced or separated woman who has not remarried.
  • **Minor Children:** Legitimate or illegitimate children who are unable to maintain themselves.
  • **Major Children:** Legitimate or illegitimate children (not being married daughters) who are physically or mentally abnormal and unable to maintain themselves.
  • **Parents:** Father or mother, unable to maintain themselves.

Basic Requirements to Claim Maintenance

The claimant must establish:

  1. **Relationship:** They fall into one of the covered categories (Wife/Child/Parent).
  2. **Inability to Maintain:** They lack sufficient means to support themselves.
  3. **Refusal/Neglect:** The respondent (husband/father/son/daughter) has sufficient means but neglects or refuses to provide maintenance.

Determining Quantum and Procedure

How is the Amount Decided?

The Magistrate's Court decides the quantum on a case-by-case basis. Key factors include:

  • The **income and financial status** (means) of the respondent.
  • The **reasonable needs** and expenses of the claimant.
  • The **standard of living** the claimant is accustomed to.
  • Any other sources of income or property the claimant may possess.

The Procedure (Summary Trial)

  1. The eligible person files a petition in the Magistrate's Court.
  2. The Court issues a notice to the respondent.
  3. The Court conducts a summary inquiry, taking oral and documentary evidence (often prioritizing speed over deep legal technicalities).
  4. If the grounds are met, the Court issues an **order** to pay monthly maintenance from the date of the application or the date of the order.

Key Facts at a Glance

Feature Details
**Nature of Proceeding** Summary Criminal (protective, quasi-civil remedy)
**Jurisdiction** Judicial Magistrate of the First Class
**Enforcement** Warrant for levying the amount, attachment of property, or imprisonment (for a maximum of one month per month's unpaid allowance)
**Modification** Orders can be modified if there is a **material change** in circumstances (Sec 127 CrPC).

Simple Examples

Example A (Wife):

A woman, separated from her employed husband, files for maintenance as she is only a homemaker with no income. The Court, finding the husband has sufficient means, orders a fixed monthly support amount.

Example B (Parents):

An elderly, ailing mother who transferred all her property to her son, now finds herself neglected and without income. She successfully claims maintenance from the adult son under this section.

Landmark Cases & Legal Principles

  • **Mohd. Ahmed Khan v. Shah Bano Begum (1985):** A landmark case that confirmed the secular nature of Section 125 CrPC, establishing its universal applicability to all citizens regardless of their personal law (though Parliament later passed the Muslim Women (Protection of Rights on Divorce) Act).
  • **Rajnesh v. Neha (2020):** The Supreme Court laid down detailed guidelines for calculating maintenance, emphasizing the need for both parties to file sworn affidavits disclosing their assets and liabilities for fair assessment.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Section 125 CrPC a Civil or Criminal Proceeding?

It is a **summary criminal proceeding**, but its objective is civil (providing financial relief). Its inclusion in the CrPC ensures quick disposal and effective enforcement mechanisms like imprisonment for non-payment.

Does a wife lose the right to maintenance if she is divorced?

No. Section 125 explicitly covers a divorced wife who has not remarried, provided she is unable to maintain herself. (Specific personal laws may offer other remedies as well).

Can the maintenance order be retrospective?

Yes. The Magistrate has the discretion to award maintenance from the **date of the order** or from the **date of the application**.
