What does Section 302 IPC say?

Section 302 of the Indian Penal Code states: "Whoever commits murder shall be punished with death or imprisonment for life, and shall also be liable to fine." In simple terms: if a person intentionally kills another person, they face either the death penalty or life imprisonment, plus a possible fine.

What counts as “murder”?

Murder is defined in Section 300 IPC. A killing is murder when:

  • There is an intention to cause death, or
  • There is an intention to cause serious bodily harm likely to cause death, or
  • The act is done with knowledge that it will probably result in death.

Punishments available

  • Death penalty — reserved for the "rarest of rare" cases.
  • Life imprisonment — typically means imprisonment for the convict's natural life unless remission is granted.
  • Fine — courts may impose a monetary penalty in addition to the sentence.

Difference: Section 302 vs Section 304

Section 302 covers murder (with clear intent/knowledge). Section 304 covers culpable homicide not amounting to murder — where intention to kill may be absent or the act happens in sudden provocation; punishment under Section 304 is generally lesser.

Simple examples

Example A (Murder): A plans and stabs B with the intention to kill. → Section 302 applies.

Example B (Culpable Homicide): A fights with B in sudden anger and hits B; B dies unexpectedly. → Likely Section 304, not Section 302.

Landmark cases

  • Bachan Singh v. State of Punjab (1980) — established the "rarest of rare" doctrine for the death penalty.
  • Machhi Singh v. State of Punjab (1983) — clarified factors for awarding capital punishment.
  • State of U.P. v. Rajesh Gautam (2003) — examined intention and circumstances in murder trials.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

What is the punishment under Section 302 IPC?
Death or life imprisonment, and the offender may also be liable to a fine.
Is Section 302 a bailable offence?
No. It is non-bailable and cognizable.
When is death penalty awarded?
Only in the "rarest of rare" cases — where the crime is exceptionally heinous or brutal, after careful judicial consideration.
