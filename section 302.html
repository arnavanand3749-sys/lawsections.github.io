Criminal Law

Section 302 IPC

Punishment for murder

What does Section 302 say?

"Whoever commits murder shall be punished with death or imprisonment for life, and shall also be liable to fine."

In simple terms: if a person intentionally kills another person, they face either the death penalty or life imprisonment, plus a possible fine.

What counts as "murder"?

Murder is technically defined in Section 300 IPC. A killing is considered murder when:

  • There is a clear intention to cause death.
  • There is an intention to cause serious bodily harm that is sufficient to cause death in the ordinary course of nature.
  • The act is done with knowledge that it is so imminently dangerous that it will typically result in death (e.g., firing a loaded cannon into a crowd).

Punishments available

  • Death Penalty: Reserved strictly for the "rarest of rare" cases.
  • Life Imprisonment: Typically means imprisonment for the convict's natural life.
  • Fine: Courts may impose a monetary penalty in addition to the sentence.

Key Facts at a Glance

Feature Details
Nature of Offence Cognizable (Police can arrest without warrant), Non-bailable, Non-compoundable
Trial Court Court of Session
Relevant Sections Section 300 (Definition), Section 302 (Punishment)

Difference: Section 302 vs 304

Section 302 covers murder (premeditated or with clear intent). Section 304 covers "Culpable Homicide not amounting to murder"—situations where intention might be absent, or the act happens in sudden provocation or heat of passion.

Simple Examples

Example A (Murder - Sec 302):

A buys a knife, waits for B to come home, and stabs B in the chest with the intention to kill. Since this is premeditated and intentional, Section 302 applies.

Example B (Culpable Homicide - Sec 304):

A and B get into a sudden argument at a bar. In a fit of rage, A pushes B; B falls, hits his head on a stone, and dies. Since there was no prior plan to kill, this is likely Culpable Homicide (Section 304), not Murder.

Landmark Supreme Court Cases

  • Bachan Singh v. State of Punjab (1980)
    Established the "rarest of rare" doctrine for awarding the death penalty.
  • Machhi Singh v. State of Punjab (1983)
    Clarified the specific factors judges must look at before awarding capital punishment.
  • K.M. Nanavati v. State of Maharashtra (1962)
    A famous case discussing "Grave and Sudden Provocation" (Murder vs Culpable Homicide).

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the punishment under Section 302 IPC?

Death or imprisonment for life, and the offender is also liable to pay a fine.

Is Section 302 a bailable offence?

No. It is non-bailable. Getting bail is extremely difficult and usually only considered by higher courts under special circumstances.

When is the death penalty awarded?

Only in the "rarest of rare" cases—where the crime is exceptionally heinous, brutal, or shocks the collective conscience of society.
